                                          Abstract

This document provides a complete, standalone mathematical specification of the Thiele Ma-
chine, a formal model of computation in which structural information carries an explicit, con-
served cost µ. The document is organized so that the machine can be reconstructed from first
principles using only the mathematics herein.
    Every logical inference has been verified by the machine-checked Coq source corpus com-
prising the formal verification suite. However, formal verification establishes that conclusions
follow from axioms—it does not validate that axioms model physical reality. This document is
therefore careful to distinguish three epistemological categories: (S) Structural theorems about
the machine’s own mathematics, (C) Conditional derivations where physics conclusions follow
from stated axioms that may or may not hold in nature, and (R) Consistency relations that
verify internal compatibility but do not constitute independent predictions.
    The specification covers: the state space and operational semantics; the core “No Free In-
sight” theorem; computational universality; gauge symmetry and Noether conservation; condi-
tional derivations connecting µ-accounting to quantum mechanics (Born rule, Tsirelson bound,
no-cloning, unitarity, complex amplitudes, Schrödinger equation) under stated physical axioms;
consistency relations (Planck’s constant); emergent spacetime and thermodynamics; the Thiele
Manifold and self-reference tower; and falsifiable predictions.

Chapter 1

Introduction

The Thiele Machine is a computational model designed to make the thermodynamic cost of
structure explicit. Unlike a Turing Machine, which treats all state transitions as cost-equivalent,
the Thiele Machine assigns a specific cost µ to every operation that extracts or asserts structural
information.
    The central claim, proven formally, is: one cannot gain structural insight about a system
without paying µ-cost. This specification explores how this constraint, combined with explicit
physical axioms (superposition, linearity, information conservation), yields structural results
that parallel the laws of quantum mechanics, thermodynamics, and emergent spacetime. The
epistemological status of each result—whether it is a structural theorem (S), a conditional
derivation (C), or a consistency relation (R)—is clearly marked throughout.
    This specification is organized into eight parts:

  1. Foundations: State space, instruction set, operational semantics.

  2. Core Theorems: No Free Insight, µ-Chaitin, cost = complexity.

  3. Computation: Universality, strict subsumption, confluence, halting.

  4. Symmetry & Conservation: µ-conservation, Noether/gauge, receipts.

  5. Quantum Mechanics: Born rule, Tsirelson, no-cloning, unitarity, complex amplitudes,
     Schrödinger, Planck.

  6. Emergent Structure: Spacetime metric, causal cones, information causality, Landauer
     bridge.

  7. Meta-Theory: Self-reference, Thiele Manifold, Genesis, three-layer isomorphism, Curry–
     Howard–Thiele.

  8. Predictions: Falsifiable divergences from standard QM.


1.1     Epistemological Framework
Formal verification (Coq) establishes that conclusions follow logically from axioms. It does
not establish that axioms model physical reality. This distinction is critical for evaluating the
physics claims in Parts V–VIII.
   Every result in this specification falls into one of three categories:

(S) Structural Theorems about the Thiele Machine as a mathematical object. These are
     unconditionally true given the definitions. Examples: µ-conservation, gauge invariance,
     confluence, halting undecidability, strict Turing subsumption.

                                                4
(C) Conditional Theorems of the form “if axiom X holds, then physics result Y follows.” The
    logical inference is verified; whether axiom X holds in nature is an empirical question.
    Examples: Born rule uniqueness (conditional on linearity), complex necessity (conditional
    on 2D amplitudes), unitarity (conditional on information conservation).

(R) Consistency Algebraic identities or definitional equivalences that verify internal coher-
    ence but do not constitute independent predictions. Examples: Planck consistency rela-
    tion, Tsirelson cost definition.

    Each major theorem in Parts V–VIII is annotated with its category. A theorem marked (C)
or (R) is not diminished—conditional derivations and consistency checks are valuable scientific
tools—but the reader should not mistake them for derivations from first principles without
stated assumptions. See Appendix B for the complete classification table.

The State Space

2.1     Primitives
Let N = {0, 1, 2, . . . }. Let B = {0, 1}. Let Σ be a finite alphabet.


2.2     Regions and Modules
Definition 2.1 (Region). A Region R is a finite subset of N, canonically represented as
a deduplicated list preserving first-occurrence order: norm(L) = nodup(L) removes duplicate
elements while preserving the order of their first appearance. (The Python VM applies a stricter
canonical form via sorted(set(region)); the Coq specification uses nodup Nat.eq dec which
does not sort.)

Definition 2.2 (Module). A Module M = (RM , AM ) consists of a normalized region RM ⊂ N
and a set of axioms AM ⊂ Σ∗ .


2.3     The Partition Graph
Definition 2.3 (Partition Graph). A partition graph G = (Nid , T ) where Nid ∈ N is the next
available module ID and T : {0, . . . , Nid − 1} ⇀ M is a finite partial function from IDs to
modules.

Axiom 2.1 (Well-Formedness). G is well-formed iff dom(T ) ⊆ {0, . . . , Nid − 1}.


2.4     Machine State
Definition 2.4 (VM State). The complete state is a 7-tuple:

                                  S = (G, C, R, Mem, PC , µ, err )

where G ∈ G is the partition graph, C ∈ N3 comprises the CSRs (certificate, status, error),
R ∈ N32 is the register file, Mem ∈ N256 is main memory, PC ∈ N is the program counter,
µ ∈ N is the µ-ledger, and err ∈ B is the global error flag.

Instruction Set and Cost Model

3.1     Instruction Categories
The instruction set I is partitioned into three categories. Every instruction i carries an explicit
cost parameter ∆µi ∈ N.

3.1.1    Structural Operations
These modify the partition graph G:

    PNEW(R, ∆µ): Create module with region R.

    PSPLIT(m, L, R, ∆µ): Split module m into disjoint regions.

    PMERGE(m1 , m2 , ∆µ): Merge two modules.

    PDISCOVER(m, E, ∆µ): Attach evidence E (list of axioms) to module m.

    MDLACC(m, ∆µ): Minimum-description-length accumulate on module m.

3.1.2    Logical Operations
These interact with information content:

    LASSERT(m, ϕ, cert, ∆µ): Assert formula ϕ on module m with certificate cert, which is ei-
     ther a SAT model (lassert cert sat) or an UNSAT LRAT proof (lassert cert unsat).

    LJOIN(c1 , c2 , ∆µ): Join certificate checksums.

    REVEAL(m, n, cert, ∆µ): Reveal n bits of structure from m. Primary source of µ-cost.

    EMIT(m, p, ∆µ): Emit payload p from module m.

3.1.3    Computational Operations (Reversible ALU)
Standard reversible register/memory operations: XFER, XOR LOAD, XOR ADD, XOR SWAP, XOR RANK.

3.1.4    Auxiliary Operations
    PYEXEC(payload , ∆µ): Execute Python payload (host bridge).

    CHSH TRIAL(x, y, a, b, ∆µ): Run a CHSH correlation trial.

    ORACLE HALTS(payload , ∆µ): Query halting oracle (requires ∆µ > 0).


                                                7
    HALT(∆µ): Halt execution.

   The full ISA comprises 18 instructions (4 structural + 4 logical + 5 ALU + 5 auxiliary).
Every instruction carries an explicit ∆µ parameter; the function instruction cost extracts it.


3.2    Cost Model
Every instruction i carries an explicit cost parameter ∆µi ∈ N. The function instruction cost(i)
extracts this parameter. The cost is applied via apply cost(s, i) = s.µ + instruction cost(i).
Since ∆µi : N, the µ-ledger is monotonically non-decreasing by construction.
   Semantically, only REVEAL and LASSERT (when adding structure) constitute genuine information-
theoretic cost. Other instructions may carry ∆µ > 0 as a bookkeeping charge, but in typical
programs their cost is set to zero.

Operational Semantics

4.1     Transition Function
The transition δ : S × I → S is defined by:

   1. PC ′ = PC + 1.

   2. µ′ = µ + ∆µi .

   3. err ′ = err ∨ fail(S, i).


4.2     Traces and Execution
Definition 4.1 (Trace). A trace τ = [i0 , . . . , ik ] ∈ I ∗ is a finite sequence of instructions.

Definition 4.2 (Execution). Run([], S) = S;            Run(i :: τ ′ , S) = Run(τ ′ , δ(S, i)).


4.3     Selected Instruction Semantics
PNEW: Normalize region; if already present, idempotent; else add (Rnorm , ∅) to G.
    REVEAL: Update CSRs with checksum of certificate cert. Cost: ∆µ (typically 1).
    LASSERT: Verify certificate cert for formula ϕ. If cert is lassert cert unsat: verify
LRAT proof (UNSAT). If cert is lassert cert sat: verify model satisfies formula (SAT). If
valid, append ϕ to Am ; if invalid, set err ← true. Replaces oracles with verifiable proofs.

No Free Insight

The central theorem: structural insight is never free.


5.1     Definitions
Definition 5.1 (Receipt Predicate). P : O → B is a computable function on observation
histories.

Definition 5.2 (Strength Ordering). P1 ≤ P2 iff ∀o. P1 (o) = 1 =⇒ P2 (o) = 1. P1 < P2 iff
P1 ≤ P2 and ∃o. P2 (o) = 1 ∧ P1 (o) = 0.

Definition 5.3 (Certification). Trace τ certifies P iff execution succeeds (err = 0), the supra-
certificate flag is set, and the output satisfies P .

Definition 5.4 (Structure Addition). HasStructureAddition(τ ) is true iff τ contains a REVEAL,
LASSERT, or equivalent operation.


5.2     The Theorem
Theorem 5.1 (No Free Insight (NoFreeInsight Theorem.v)). Let Pstrong < Pweak . If a trace τ
starting from a clean state s0 certifies Pstrong , then:

                       Certified(τ, Pstrong ) =⇒ HasStructureAddition(τ )

Proof Sketch. The proof is parametric: NoFreeInsight Theorem.v proves the theorem for any
system satisfying the NO FREE INSIGHT SYSTEM module type interface. The interface requires
four contract obligations (not Coq Axiom declarations—they are proved separately for the kernel
instantiation): (1) µ-monotonicity, (2) a certification specification linking certifies to execu-
tion, (3) a no-free-insight contract stating that strict strengthening requires structure addition,
and (4) that the system has a notion of clean start. The theorem file itself contains zero Axiom,
zero Admitted. The contract obligations are discharged by the kernel instantiation.

Corollary 5.2 (Cost of Insight). Gaining insight (strengthening the predicate) implies ∆µ > 0.

The µ-Chaitin Theorem

A Chaitin-style incompleteness theorem denominated in µ-currency.

Theorem 6.1 (µ-Chaitin (MuChaitinTheory Theorem.v)). For any formal theory system T
satisfying the µ-Chaitin interface, the number of bits k the theory can certify is bounded by:

                                          k ≤ |T | + c

where |T | is the description length of the theory and c is a fixed overhead constant. The proof
chain: k ≤ payload ≤ µ-info ≤ budget ≤ |T | + c.

Cost Equals Kolmogorov Complexity

Theorem 7.1 (µ-Bits = Prefix-Free Complexity (CostIsComplexity.v)). Let K(spec) denote
the prefix-free Kolmogorov complexity of specification spec. Then:

                                     µ(spec) = K(spec)

Specifically, µ(spec) = |spec| + 1 (the terminating bit), which equals the minimum description
length over all prefix-free programs producing spec.

Proof. The canonical compiler maps spec 7→ spec∥[true]. Any producing program has the form
p = spec∥[true] with |p| = |spec| + 1. The compiler achieves the minimum, and µ counts exactly
these bits.

No Free Lunch and No Arbitrage

8.1     No Free Lunch (Ghosts Are Impossible)
Theorem 8.1 (No Free Lunch (NoFreeLunch.v)). A “ghost” is defined as two distinct propo-
sitions p ̸= q represented by the same physical state. Ghosts are impossible:

                       ∀ p ̸= q, ∄ s s.t. s faithfully represents both p and q

Information cannot exist without physical distinction.


8.2     No Arbitrage Implies Potential Function
Theorem 8.2 (Potential from No-Arbitrage (NoArbitrage.v)). Let w : I ∗ → Z be a cost
function satisfying:

  1. Additivity: w([]) = 0 and w(τ1 · τ2 ) = w(τ1 ) + w(τ2 ).

  2. No-Arbitrage: For every closed cycle τ (returning to the starting state), w(τ ) ≥ 0.

Then there exists a potential function ϕ : S → Z such that:

                                   w(τ ) ≥ ϕ(apply(τ, s)) − ϕ(s)

for every state s and trace τ .

Remark 8.1. This is structurally analogous to the Second Law of Thermodynamics: consistent
cost accounting implies a potential function bounding all transitions. The concrete Coq model
is a toy (increment/decrement on nat with asymmetric costs). No temperature, entropy, or
thermodynamic system appears in the formalization. The connection to thermodynamics is an
analogy, not a derivation.

9.2    Strict Containment
Theorem 9.2 (Turing ⊊ Thiele (Subsumption.v)). Classical Turing computation is strictly
contained in sighted Thiele computation:

                                     ∃ p. sighted(p) ∧ ¬turing(p)

There exist programs that are Thiele-computable (using partition structure and µ-accounting)
but are not classical Turing programs. The extra structure is genuine new content.

Theorem 9.3 (Semantic Strictness (ThieleFoundations.v)). There exist Thiele traces with iden-
tical Turing shadows but non-isomorphic Thiele-level behavior. The partition and µ layers carry
information not present in the Turing skeleton.


9.3    Halting Undecidability
Theorem 9.4 (Diagonal Argument (kernel/OracleImpossibility.v)). No total computable func-
tion decides the halting problem for the Thiele Machine.


9.4    Oracle Cost Lower Bound
Theorem 9.5 (Oracle Impossibility (kernel/OracleImpossibility.v)). The formalization defines
a sound oracle as one charging ∆µ ≥ 1 per query (sound oracle cost). Given this definition:

                                                    14
  1. oracle halts costs mu: A zero-cost oracle violates the soundness definition (0 ≥ 1 ⇒
     ⊥).

  2. oracle cost linear: Given sound oracle cost, n queries cost ≥ n.

These results verify the internal consistency of the pricing definition. The physical justification
for why oracle queries should cost µ > 0 comes from the No Free Insight framework (gaining
information about undecidable propositions should cost something), but the Coq proofs verify the
narrower claim that the pricing is self-consistent.


9.5     Confluence
Theorem 9.6 (Church–Rosser (Confluence.v)). For any state s and two independent certificates
c1 , c2 (targeting distinct CNF formulas), applying them in either order yields the same µ-cost:

                                            µ(s12 ) = µ(s21 )

Independent structural operations commute.


9.6     Tensoriality
Theorem 9.7 (Module Independence (ThieleUnificationTensor.v)). For distinct modules m1 ̸=
m2 , recording discoveries commutes:

             record(record(G, m1 , e1 ), m2 , e2 ) ≡ext record(record(G, m2 , e2 ), m1 , e1 )

Local per-module operations are tensorial.


9.7     Amortized Analysis
Theorem 9.8 (Amortization (AmortizedAnalysis.v)). For a batch of T instances with discovery
cost D and operational cost O per instance:

                                 total cost = B · D + T · O ≥ T · O

where B is batch count. As T → ∞, average cost → O (discovery overhead amortizes away).

Gauge Symmetry and Noether’s
Theorem

11.1     The Z-Action (Gauge Group)
Definition 11.1 (Gauge Shift). For δ ∈ Z, the gauge shift σδ : S → S maps S 7→ S[µ ← µ + δ],
preserving all other fields.
Theorem 11.1 (Group Action (KernelNoether.v)). The gauge shifts form a Z-action on states:
  1. Identity: σ0 (S) = S.

  2. Composition: σa (σb (S)) = σa+b (S).

  3. Inverse: σ−n (σn (S)) = S (when µ + n ≥ 0).


11.2     Gauge Invariance
Definition 11.2 (Observable). Obs(S) extracts the partition region structure, ignoring µ.
Theorem 11.2 (Gauge Invariance (KernelNoether.v)). Obs(σδ (S)) = Obs(S). Shifting µ by a
constant does not affect observables.
Theorem 11.3 (Noether’s Theorem (KernelNoether.v, RepresentationTheorem.v)).
  1. Forward: States identical except for µ lie on the same gauge orbit. (Trivially true by
     construction: if all other fields match, the µ difference is the gauge shift.)

  2. Gauge Invariance: z gauge invariance proves that shifting µ does not change Observable partition
     This is definitional: z gauge shift only modifies the vm mu field, and Observable partition
     reads only vm graph.

  3. Trace Preservation: Gauge-equivalent states produce identical observable traces (same
     labels, same µ-costs) for any finite horizon. Proven as vm step orbit equiv.
The µ-ledger is a gauge degree of freedom. Its absolute value is unobservable; only differences
∆µ are physical. The “Noether” label is a deliberate analogy—the result follows from the record
structure (separate fields for µ and graph), not from a deep symmetry principle.
Theorem 11.4 (µ-Monotonicity (KernelNoether.v)). vm step(S, i, S ′ ) =⇒ µ(S) ≤ µ(S ′ ).
The µ-ledger never decreases under the dynamics. This is true by construction: instruction
costs are natural numbers (≥ 0), so adding them to the ledger cannot decrease it. The proof
examines each step constructor and confirms the arithmetic.

Two-Dimensional Amplitude Space

Remark 12.1 (Epistemological Status: (C) Conditional Derivation). The following derivation
assumes that partition states admit amplitude representations (superpositions), not merely
classical probability distributions. This is stated as Axiom 12.1. Without it, classical probability
theory (p ∈ [0, 1], one-dimensional, continuous) suffices and the 2D argument does not apply.
