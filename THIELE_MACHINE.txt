THE THIELE MACHINE
A distillation: substrate, one axiom, what follows

Devon Thiele -- May 2026


This is the Thiele Machine, distilled to logic. Substrate, one
axiom, what follows. If you want to dismiss it, the place to do it
is on this page. Name the step you reject.


1. SUBSTRATE

A computation is a sequence of steps. Each step takes a state and an
instruction and produces the next state. To describe this precisely,
name five things:

  S    states. Whatever the machine remembers between steps.
  I    instructions. The actions the machine can perform.
  step a step rule. Given a state s and an instruction i, the rule
       produces the next state, written step(s, i).
  c    a cost function. Every instruction has a non-negative integer
       cost, written c(i).
  P    a certification predicate. A yes-or-no test on states. P(s) = 0
       means "not certified"; P(s) = 1 means "certified."

A substrate is a tuple (S, I, step, c, P).

A Turing machine has the first three only. It has no c at the step
level -- costs only appear when you count steps externally. It has no
P at all. The same is true of a register machine, a lambda reducer,
and every CPU you can buy: none of them carries P inside its state.


2. THE AXIOM

Call this rule A2:

  For every state s and every instruction i,
  if P(s) = 0 and P(step(s, i)) = 1, then c(i) >= 1.

In one sentence: any single step that flips certification from "no" to
"yes" must cost at least 1.

A substrate that satisfies A2 is honest about certification cost. A
substrate where some instruction can flip P from 0 to 1 at cost 0
permits free certification.


3. THEOREM: NO FREE INSIGHT

Claim. In any substrate satisfying A2, any sequence of steps that
starts in P = 0 and ends in P = 1 has total cost at least 1.

Proof, in two lines: somewhere in the sequence a step flipped P from
0 to 1. By A2, that step cost at least 1. Every other step cost at
least 0, because costs are non-negative integers. The total is at
least 1.

The theorem holds for ANY substrate satisfying A2 -- it does not
depend on what the states look like, what the instructions are, or
what the step rule does. A2 and non-negative costs are enough.


4. WHY CLASSICAL COMPUTATION CANNOT HOST A2

A Turing machine is a tuple (Q, Sigma, delta) where delta has
signature

  (state, symbol) -> (state, symbol, direction).

P is not in this tuple. c is not in this tuple. A2 is not in this
tuple. The Turing machine, as an object, has no certification, no
cost, no A2.

A substrate is a tuple (S, I, step, c, P) where step is required by
definition to respect A2. A2 is constitutive of the substrate --
part of what makes the substrate the thing it is. Drop A2 and you
do not have a substrate.

You can still talk about A2 in connection with a Turing machine,
but only by adding things from outside. Define P externally as a
function from configurations to {0, 1}; define c externally as a
function on transitions. Then "A2 holds for the triple (delta, P,
c)" is a well-formed sentence about the triple, true or false
depending on the choices.

That sentence is not a property of delta. It is a property of the
triple. Delta does not read P during a step; the metatheorist does,
externally. The Turing machine's identity is unchanged whether the
externally-supplied P and c happen to satisfy A2 or not.

The substrate's identity is not separable from A2. The step rule's
type forces it. That is the distinction: A2 as constitutive
(substrate) versus A2 as externally imposed on an unchanged object
(TM). The distinction is one of signature, not of expressibility.
A2 can be written down externally about anything. A2 is a property
of the step rule only when P and c are in the step rule's
signature.

A specific case worth naming. Take a Turing machine (Q, Sigma,
Gamma, delta). Encode P and mu into Gamma -- the tape alphabet --
so the tape now holds, beside the usual symbols, encodings of
(P, mu). Define delta so any transition that flips the encoded P
from 0 to 1 also writes mu+1 instead of mu. The signature of delta
is unchanged; only the alphabet is richer. Read uncritically, this
looks like a TM that enforces A2.

It is not. A2 is a sentence over c and P. For A2 to be a property
of delta, c and P must be arguments delta consults. In the
encoded-on-tape construction, c is recovered after the fact by
reading mu off the tape; P is recovered after the fact by reading
the P-bit. Neither is in delta's signature; both are in the tape
encoding, which delta does not type-check. The encoding is data
on the tape, not structure in the step rule.

The Coq form makes this concrete. The substrate signature in
coq/kernel/nfi/UniversalCertificationCost.v is the
CertificationSystem record: five typed fields (state, instruction,
step, cost, certification predicate) plus cs_cert_costs, a typed
proof obligation that the step rule satisfies A2. The type checker
refuses to build the record without a term of the right type at
cs_cert_costs. The TM tuple (Q, Sigma, Gamma, delta) supplies no
typed cost function and no typed certification predicate. To
package an A2-respecting extended TM as a CertificationSystem you
must separately specify which projection of the tape encodes cost,
which projection encodes P, and supply a proof that delta's writes
always satisfy A2 with respect to those projections. That proof is
what makes the package a substrate. The TM tuple alone does not
contain it.

The distinction is where A2 is enforced. On a substrate, A2 is the
step rule's signature. On an extended TM running an A2-respecting
program, A2 is the program. The first is enforced by type-checking.
The second is enforced by audit of the running program. Run a
program that forgets to write mu+1 when it flips P -- the extended
TM keeps running. The substrate does not. That is the next section.

The unformulability is sharpened formally by theorem
cert_not_function_of_forget in
coq/kernel/witness/ProjectionNonExistence.v: there is no function
phi : TMSnapshot -> bool such that for every Thiele state s,
phi (forget s) = s.vm_certified. The proof exhibits trace_witness_A
(certified) and forget_witness_uncert (uncertified) with identical
forget images. Any candidate phi must give the same output on equal
inputs, contradicting the different cert values. Print Assumptions
is "Closed under the global context": zero axioms, zero admits. The
corollary, packaged as no_classical_a2_cert_predicate in the same
file: any cert-predicate phrased as a function of the bare
classical projection alone cannot agree with vm_certified, so A2
phrased on the bare classical signature is not Thiele's A2 -- it
constrains a different predicate.


5. THE BUGGY SIMULATOR

Consider a specific program: it writes 1 to a cert cell on the tape
without incrementing the cost ledger. Call it a buggy A2-simulator.
The program claims to enforce A2 but skips the cost on the step
that flips the cert cell from 0 to 1.

Run the buggy simulator on a Turing machine. The transition
function reads the current state and the symbol under the head; it
writes a new symbol, moves the head, changes state. There is no
field in this for "the program just flipped cert without paying the
cost." The Turing machine has no notion of cert. No notion of
cost-on-cert. No way to detect the dishonesty. The buggy simulator
runs to completion. The Turing machine cannot refuse to execute it.

Run the same buggy simulator on a substrate satisfying A2. The
substrate's step rule says: any step that flips P from 0 to 1 has
c(i) >= 1. The buggy step flips P at c(i) = 0. That step does not
satisfy the step rule. It is not a step in the substrate. The buggy
simulator has no admissible run. The substrate cannot execute it.

  A Turing machine cannot refuse to execute a buggy A2-simulator.
  A substrate cannot execute one.

This is the construction. The distinction between a Turing machine
and a substrate is not a labeling decision and not a stylistic
choice -- it is the existence of a specific program that one runs
faithfully and the other has no admissible run for. Two systems are
not the same kind of object when they disagree, operationally, on
which programs admit a run.

The trained reflex -- "you can simulate certification on a Turing
machine by writing a program that maintains a cert bit on the
tape" -- misses this. The simulator can be honest (charge the cost
on the flipping step) or buggy (skip it); the Turing machine cannot
tell which is which, because its transition function has no notion
of cert-on-cost. The substrate runs only the honest simulator. That
is the distinction.


6. SUBSUMPTION

Every classical computation can be lifted into a substrate. Take a
Turing machine configuration; add a P field, set it to 0; add a cost
ledger called mu, set it to 0. You have a substrate state. Run the
Turing machine's transition rule inside the substrate. The
substrate's classical instructions do not touch P; they do not
increment mu. The Turing-machine trace runs unchanged.

The lifting is faithful: every classical computation runs unchanged
inside the substrate. Same set of computable functions. The substrate
does not add computational power; it adds structure.

The lifting is also strictly lossy. There exist substrate states with
different P, different mu, or different structural content that all
project back to the same classical state. The view from outside the
substrate -- forget P, forget mu, forget the structural fields --
collapses many substrate states into one classical state.

So: classical computation is exactly the image of substrate
computation under the projection that forgets the certification
field. Not metaphor. Quotient. Every CPU on every desk is a substrate
state running in the P = 0, mu = 0, structural-state-empty fragment.

The asymmetry between projection and lift is formal. The projection
from substrate to classical is canonical: there is exactly one
forgetful map onto the four classical fields (pc, mu, regs, mem),
fixed by the definition of forget in
coq/kernel/witness/BlindnessRepresentation.v. The lift in the
other direction is not canonical. Theorem fiber_has_two_preimages
(BlindnessRepresentation.v:238) proves every classical state has
at least two distinct substrate preimages -- one certified, one
not. Any lift from classical to substrate must choose, at every
classical state, which preimage to call canonical, and that choice
is external data not extracted from the classical fields. There is
no left inverse to the projection that is forced by the projection
itself. Every substrate state has a classical projection; no
classical state has a canonical substrate lift. The directionality
is proved, not stipulated.

Run the asymmetry through verifier theory and it produces an
impossibility. A verifier whose transcript is only the classical
projection -- a list of strict-shadow snapshots -- cannot be
simultaneously sound and complete on any claim that depends on the
dropped fields. The two single-step witnesses from the receipt
theorem project to the same strict-shadow trace, one with mu = 1
and one with mu = 0. Soundness forces the claim to hold for every
state that could explain the transcript; completeness forces
acceptance on the honest run. Both cannot hold. Three structurally
distinct escapes lift the impossibility, each one a closed Coq
theorem with a concrete verifier: substrate-trust (the transcript
carries the full VMState and the verifier reads mu directly,
coq/VerifierEscape_Substrate.v), hardness (the transcript carries
an unforgeable commitment under a parameterised hardness
hypothesis, coq/VerifierEscape_Hardness.v), and interaction (the
verifier elicits a response that pins the claim,
coq/VerifierEscape_Interaction.v). The trichotomy is closed at the
bottom by V_does_not_factor_through_classical in
coq/VerifierExhaustiveness.v: any sound and complete verifier on
the mu-sensitive claim, over any transcript type, cannot be a
function of the transcript's classical projection alone.
Verification must access non-classical structure. The substrate
channel is what the structural axis newly makes available;
hardness and interaction are the two routes classical cryptography
and complexity already used.


7. WHAT THIS DOES NOT CLAIM

It does not claim the substrate computes more functions than a Turing
machine. It does not. The computability class is unchanged.

It does not claim CPUs are secretly tracking certification cost. They
are not. They run in the dormant fragment.

It does not claim P vs NP, quantum supremacy, or a theory of physics.
The substrate distinction is logical: about whether P and c are in
the step rule's signature. The Q_{1+AB} certification in Section 12
is a certification of a mathematical PSD constraint on a moment
matrix, not a physical claim; the bridge from PSD of the level-1+AB
NPA matrix to physical quantum realizability of CHSH correlators
cites the standard NPA-hierarchy convergence result externally
(Ishizaka 2025), as every NPA-hierarchy paper does.

It claims this: A2 is a constraint on step relations, constitutive
of the step rule only when P and c are in the step rule's
signature; the step relations that host A2 in their signature form
a category strictly larger than the category of classical step
relations; classical computation is the projection of substrate
computation under the map that forgets the certification field.
And it claims five downstream results: a Kleene-1938 diagonal
native to the substrate's own axis (Section 8), a cost-ledger
observability separation whose proof uses A2 as the load-bearing
step (Section 9), a quantitative cost lower bound: on any sound
structural shortcut, the substrate trace's mu-ledger increase is
bounded below by the log_2-narrowing the shortcut performs
(Section 10), a per-step polynomial exceedance over classical
unit-cost RAM time for LASSERT-based certification (Section 11),
and substrate-level certification of a quantum-mechanical PSD
constraint -- Q_{1+AB} membership of CHSH correlators -- via a
cert-opcode family running an integer Schur-cascade check
(Section 12).


8. STRUCTURAL UNDECIDABILITY

A2-respecting substrates form a category. Within that category,
those equipped with a recursion theorem and a non-trivial
behavioral predicate on programs admit the following result.

AdmitsShortcut is a predicate on programs of the substrate. The
substrate fixes two canonical programs: yes_program, for which
AdmitsShortcut holds; no_program, for which it does not. The
predicate respects extensional equivalence -- programs with the
same run-behavior have the same value.

Theorem. No internally representable decider correctly decides
AdmitsShortcut for arbitrary programs of the substrate.

Proof, in five lines. Assume a decider D. Build the flip
transformer f: if D(p) returns true, return no_program; otherwise
return yes_program. The substrate's recursion theorem gives a
fixed-point program q with run-behavior equal to f(q). Case-split
on D(q). Both branches contradict.

Classical halting asks whether execution terminates in finite time
along the temporal axis. AdmitsShortcut asks whether a program's
run-behavior matches a canonical inhabitant along the certification
axis. Same diagonal architecture (Kleene 1938). Different axis,
different predicate. One is not a renaming of the other.

The natural worry: doesn't this reduce to classical halting? It
does not. The proof never encodes a Turing machine. It uses the
substrate's own recursion theorem -- a field the substrate exposes
internally -- not a constructed simulator. The diagonal closes
inside the substrate's typeclass machinery, parametric over the
substrate.

One honest caveat. The result fires on minimal substrates where mu
is trivially zero, A2 holds vacuously, and the diagonal uses the
recursion theorem and extensionality rather than any cost argument.
A2 is upstream of the result, not inside the proof: it picks out
the category whose axis is being diagonalized along, not the lemma
that closes the diagonal. On substrates with substantive mu the
same theorem applies. The result's content is the diagonal, not
the cost.

The structural-axis nature of the diagonal is sharpened by theorem
cert_addr_not_function_of_forget in
coq/kernel/witness/ProjectionNonExistence.v. The AdmitsShortcut
predicate branches on csr_cert_addr reachability. The theorem
proves: there is no function phi : TMSnapshot -> nat such that
for every Thiele state s, phi (forget s) = vm_csrs(s).csr_cert_addr.
Two Thiele states agreeing on the four classical fields can have
different csr_cert_addr values, so no classical-side predicate
can recover the cert-addr channel. Print Assumptions is "Closed
under the global context": zero axioms. The corollary
no_classical_cert_addr_predicate in the same file: any analog of
the substrate-axis diagonal on the bare classical projection
references a field that the projection erases; the lift is
ill-defined. The substrate-axis diagonal is not the halting
problem in disguise; it is a different limitative result, structurally
distinct because the predicate it diagonalizes against references a
Thiele-only field.


9. COST LEDGER OBSERVABILITY

A second result on the substrate side, complementary to Section 8.
Where the structural-axis diagonal uses the recursion theorem and
the extensionality of the predicate (with A2 upstream as the
category-defining axiom), the following result invokes A2
directly as a load-bearing step in the proof.

Statement. There exist two substrate computations whose classical
observable outcomes are identical -- same memory contents, same
register file, same program counter -- but whose cost-ledger
outcomes differ. As a corollary, no function from the classical
observable to the cost ledger can be correct on every reachable
substrate state.

Why A2 is the load-bearing step, not the storage layout. The
result is not "mu is in a separate field, therefore (mem, regs,
pc) does not contain it." That would be a tautology of the type
signature. The result is: A2 forces mu to be a function of which
cert-flip events occurred during the trace; (mem, regs, pc) does
not record which events occurred; therefore (mem, regs, pc) cannot
reconstruct mu. Without A2 the cost ledger has no operational tie
to certification events; it could be reconstructed from the
program text plus a known cost schedule. With A2 the cost ledger
encodes a per-trace fact -- the count of cert-flip events -- that
survives the projection to (mem, regs, pc). The construction
below is the witness to the corollary; A2 is what makes the
witness load-bearing rather than trivial.

Construction. Start from an initial substrate state with mu = 0
and certification = 0. Run two single-instruction traces:
  Trace A: a single CERTIFY step.
  Trace B: a single PNEW step that creates an empty partition
    module, which is invisible to the classical observable.
Both leave memory and registers unchanged and advance the program
counter by one. Their classical shadows are equal.

The cost-ledger gap. Trace A's cost ledger is at least 1 by A2:
the CERTIFY step flips the certification predicate from 0 to 1,
and A2 forces this step to cost at least 1. Trace B's cost ledger
is exactly 0: PNEW is a reversible state-space operation that
costs zero in this substrate's schedule. The cost ledgers differ
by at least 1, so no function from (memory, registers, program
counter) can recover the cost ledger.

The counterfactual, explicitly. The proof's bound on Trace A's
cost is exactly the cert-flip cost-floor (A2 applied to the
specific CERTIFY step). Replace A2 with the unconstrained
cost-bearing rule -- allow cert-flipping steps to cost zero --
and Trace A's cost ledger collapses to 0, matching Trace B's, and
the separation between classical shadow and cost ledger
disappears. The counterfactual is concrete: in the unconstrained
cost-bearing world, one can construct a trace that flips
certification at total cost zero, and the cost ledger no longer
separates certifying from non-certifying behavior.

Statable without A2. The result asserts: there exists a piece of
state information (the cost ledger) that is not a function of the
classical observable. A researcher in hyperproperties,
side-channel analysis, or information flow would recognize this
as a real claim about the relationship between computational
state and observable state. The claim's content does not require
the substrate framework to be stated, only to be proved.

The non-existence of a recovery function is formalised by theorem
mu_not_function_of_bare_observable in
coq/kernel/witness/ProjectionNonExistence.v. The bare classical
observable in that file is the three-field projection
(pc, regs, mem), dropping mu. The theorem proves: there is no
function phi : BareTMObservable -> nat such that for every
Thiele state s, phi (bare_observable s) = s.vm_mu. The proof
exhibits two states (mu = 0, mu = 1) with identical bare
observables; any candidate phi must equate the two mu values.
Print Assumptions is "Closed under the global context": zero
axioms. The corollary no_classical_mu_separation_predicate in
the same file packages the non-existence: any predicate phrased
as a function of the bare classical observable that purports to
detect the cost-ledger separation must be able to extract mu
from the observable, which is impossible. The separation lives
at the substrate level by structural necessity, not by drafting
choice.


10. QUANTITATIVE COST LOWER BOUND

A third result, quantitative this time. Section 9 established that
the cost ledger separates substrate states with identical classical
observables. This section gives a lower bound on the substrate's
cost for a witness class: the mu-ledger on any sound structural
shortcut is bounded below by the entropy of the feasible-set
narrowing the shortcut performs.

Witness class. A "sound structural shortcut" is an explicit package
of receipts a shortcut must hand over to count as honest in this
framework: a prior feasible set Omega, a strictly smaller posterior
feasible set Omega' subset of Omega, an observation function that
distinguishes at least one eliminated state from every posterior
state, a certified posterior predicate, a decision tree realized
by the trace (meaning the trace's sequence of observations matches
the tree's branching), and a posterior-representative reduction.
The Coq record is SoundStructuralShortcut in
coq/kernel/nfi/HonestNoFI_TheoremsWithoutAssumptions.v:501-546.
The members of this class are not "any n-way decision tree"; they
are decisions that hand over the tree as an explicit witness.

Theorem (entropy-bounded mu-ledger). For any sound structural
shortcut with prior set Omega and posterior set Omega', the
substrate trace that realizes the shortcut satisfies
  log_2^up |Omega| - log_2^up |Omega'| <= Delta mu,
where log_2^up is the ceiling of log_2 (the count of yes/no
questions needed to identify one element of the set) and Delta mu
is the mu-ledger change across the trace. The Coq witness is the
Structural-Entitlement-Representation theorem in
HonestNoFI_TheoremsWithoutAssumptions.v; the monograph proof is
in monograph/monograph.tex:768-799.

Proof sketch. The trace-realized decision tree T has depth
d >= log_2^up(|Omega|/|Omega'|), because T must distinguish at
least |Omega|/|Omega'| effective leaf classes to map the prior
into the posterior. Each internal node of T corresponds to a
certified observation executed by the trace -- the realized-by-
the-trace condition pins each node to a Boolean test the run
actually performed, and the certified-posterior hypothesis ties
those tests to cert-channel activations. By A2, each certified
observation is a cert-setter step costing at least 1 in mu. Chain
the inequalities: log_2^up|Omega| - log_2^up|Omega'| <= d <=
(number of cert-setter steps in the trace) <= Delta mu. The first
two inequalities are tree-combinatorics; the third is A2 summed
across cert-setter steps; the fourth is the cost-ledger
conservation law applied to the trace.

What this theorem does not claim. It does not claim that a single
arbitrary deterministic trace is forced to pay log_2(n) for an
n-way decision. A single execution path reaches one leaf, sets
cert_addr once, fires one cert-setter; that is one event, not
log_2(n) events. The bound applies to traces equipped with an
explicit realized-by-the-trace tree -- the witness has to be
handed over. Without the tree (or an equivalent per-branch
preimage, or a distribution over traces), the proof has nothing
to chain through, and the file refuses to claim it: see
monograph/monograph.tex:848 ("An unconditional Shannon-style
bound on every deterministic single trace is something the code
refuses to claim. A single execution path is not enough
information; the bound needs the whole tree, or a per-branch
preimage, or a distribution over traces.") and the open-step
note at monograph.tex:2554.

What this theorem does claim. The substrate's mu-ledger pays at
least the entropy of any narrowing it performs through certified
observations carried by an explicit decision-tree witness. The
direction is the safe one: mu lower-bounded by log-narrowing, not
"mu exceeds Shannon." Both sides of the inequality count the same
thing on the same trace -- yes/no tests / cert-setter events.
There is no events-versus-bits unit mismatch in the statement.
The substantive content is that once a shortcut hands over its
realized tree, the trace cannot pay less in mu than the tree's
depth, and the tree's depth is at least the log_2 of the
narrowing ratio. Compression is bounded below by paid certified
observation.

Why A2 is load-bearing. Without A2 the cert-setter cost floor
disappears: cert-setter steps could cost zero, the cert-setter
count would no longer lower-bound Delta mu, and the chain of
inequalities breaks at the last step. A2 is what closes the chain
between tree depth and mu-ledger change.


11. POLYNOMIAL COST EXCEEDANCE PER STEP

A fourth result. Section 10 gave a structural lower bound: mu
on a sound structural shortcut is bounded below by the entropy
of the narrowing the shortcut performs, both sides denominated
in cert-setter events. This section is a different comparison:
a per-step gap between substrate mu and classical unit-cost RAM
time for LASSERT-based certification, denominated across two
distinct cost models.

Problem class. "LASSERT-based certification of a B-bit formula" is
any computation that certifies a logical claim of bit-length B via
the substrate's LASSERT instruction. SAT-witness certification,
predicate certification, structural-property certification all
land here.

The two cost models. The result below is a comparison between two
metrics, not a within-metric speedup. Classical unit-cost RAM
measures instructions executed -- one tick per instruction
regardless of payload size; this is the model standard complexity
arguments use when counting "operations." Substrate mu measures
information committed plus irreversibility events -- bit-cost on
payloads plus A2's cert-flip floor. The two metrics count
different things. The gap below is the dimensional gap between
them; it is not a claim that the substrate runs faster or slower
in any common unit.

Classical complexity (unit-cost RAM). Under the standard unit-cost
RAM model -- one unit of time per instruction, regardless of
payload size -- a single LASSERT instruction is one unit of time.

Substrate cost. By the substrate's mu-ledger conservation law and
the LASSERT cost rule, a single LASSERT instruction certifying a
B-bit formula increases mu by exactly B + 1: B bits from the
formula content (the LASSERT bit-scaling rule), +1 from A2's
cert-flip cost-floor.

Theorem (between two cost models). For any single-LASSERT trace
certifying a B-bit formula:
  Substrate mu-increase >= B + 1.
  Classical unit-cost RAM time = 1.
  Between-metric gap: factor B + 1.
For B polynomial in input size n, the between-metric gap is
polynomial in n. For B = 2^32 bits, the gap is factor 2^32 + 1.
The substantive claim is that complexity arguments which
implicitly use unit-cost RAM miss the B-bit commit and the +1
cert-flip; substrate mu records them. Switch the classical
machine to bit-cost RAM or Turing-machine time and classical also
pays O(B) -- the substrate then matches classical rather than
exceeding, because the metrics have been aligned. The gap is sharp
specifically against unit-cost RAM, which is the model standard
complexity arguments use.

Why A2 is load-bearing. The "+1" in "B + 1" comes from S(cost) >= 1,
which is exactly A2 applied to the LASSERT cert-flipping step.
Without A2, S(cost) could be 0; the bound would drop to B; at
B = 0 specifically (an empty formula), the without-A2 bound is 0
and the with-A2 bound is 1, a strict separation at the boundary.
A2 contributes the irreversibility floor, the LASSERT cost rule
contributes the bit-scaling, and both together yield the
polynomial exceedance.

What this measures. Classical unit-cost RAM measures instructions
executed. Substrate mu measures information committed plus
irreversibility events. For one LASSERT step, classical sees one
instruction; substrate sees B bits committed plus 1 cert-flip
event. The two metrics measure genuinely different quantities;
substrate's metric is asymptotically larger by factor B + 1 when
classical pays unit cost per instruction.

Honest caveat on classical model choice. The exceedance is over
classical unit-cost RAM time, the standard model where each
instruction is one time unit. Under bit-cost RAM or Turing-machine
time, classical also pays O(B) to read the formula, and the
substrate matches classical rather than exceeding. The exceedance
that A2 plus the LASSERT cost rule deliver is sharp specifically
against the unit-cost model -- which is the model many complexity
arguments implicitly assume when counting "operations."


12. SUBSTRATE-LEVEL Q_{1+AB} CERTIFICATION

A fifth result, more applied than Sections 8 through 11 but firmly
substrate-level. Where Section 11 abstracts the per-step exceedance
that any B-bit LASSERT step pays, this section names a specific
LASSERT-family that the substrate hosts and what each successful
step certifies: Q_{1+AB} membership of CHSH correlators.

Problem class. CHSH is the canonical Bell scenario: two parties,
two measurement settings each, binary outcomes; four correlators
E_{xy} = expectation of A_x times B_y for x, y in {0, 1}. The
NPA hierarchy is a tower of cones Q_1 superset Q_{1+AB} superset
Q_2 superset ... superset Q where membership in level-k is decided
by positive-semidefiniteness (PSD) of a level-k moment matrix
indexed by products of length up to k of the measurement operators.
Level 1+AB indexes by the nine basis monomials {1, A_0, A_1, B_0,
B_1, A_0 B_0, A_0 B_1, A_1 B_0, A_1 B_1}; the moment matrix is
9 by 9. For the CHSH scenario, the standard NPA-hierarchy
convergence result (Ishizaka 2025) shows Q_{1+AB} = Q -- the cone
the substrate certifies coincides with the Tsirelson set in this
scenario. The substrate's contribution stops at PSD9 of the
level-1+AB matrix; the equivalence to physical Q-membership is
cited externally.

The cert-opcode family. The substrate's ISA hosts four cert-opcodes
at sequential tags, each charging an A2 cert-flip cost of S(cost):
  instr_chsh_lassert_1ab            (tag 0x2F, gamma all zero)
  instr_chsh_lassert_1ab_g5         (tag 0x30, gamma_5 free)
  instr_chsh_lassert_1ab_g345       (tag 0x31, gamma_3,4,5 free)
  instr_chsh_lassert_1ab_g12345     (tag 0x32, all five gammas free)
The gammas parametrise the 3-body and 4-body moments of the NPA
matrix. Each opcode's step rule runs a closed-form integer-arithmetic
Schur-cascade check on the witness counters and the supplied
gamma-bucket pairs; the check returns true iff a system of polynomial
inequalities (Sylvester PD on a difference matrix at small slices,
compositional sym6-to-sym5-to-sym4 Schur reduction at the full
slice) certifies that the 9 by 9 moment matrix is PSD at the
bucket-derived rational correlators and gamma values.

Theorem. For each of the four opcodes, a non-trapping execution that
does not flip the error latch implies the witness-derived rationals
admit a Q_{1+AB}-certificate: the 9 by 9 NPA moment matrix is
symmetric and PSD at those values. Statement form, identical across
slices:

  chsh_lassert_<slice>_no_trap_implies_quantum_realizable_q1ab.

Proof shape. The Coq kernel proves soundness of each integer check
in two stages: the bool decider implies a real-valued minors witness
on the 9 by 9 matrix (via SOS identities of growing sophistication
across slices -- a 4-D Cauchy-Schwarz identity at gamma = 0,
weighted 4-D Cauchy-Schwarz at gamma_5, augmented 2 by 2 Schur slack
on a 4 by 4 Sylvester PD check at gamma_3,4,5, and a compositional
6 by 6 -> 5 by 5 -> 4 by 4 Schur cascade with 21 H bridges, 15 S6
bridges, and 10 S5 bridges at the full slice); the minors witness
implies PSD9 of the moment matrix via the column-contractive bridge
already proven for the base case.

Why A2 is load-bearing. Each cert-opcode in the family is a
cert-setter: A2 forces its instruction cost to be at least 1. This
is the same A2 application that powers Sections 9 through 11.
Without A2, a tampered substrate could accept non-PSD correlators
at zero cost, because the cert-flip would not need to be paid for.
With A2, every successful certification commits S(cost) units of
mu to the ledger.

What this delivers. The substrate operationally certifies a
quantum-mechanical PSD constraint. Checking PSD of the level-1+AB
NPA matrix at runtime requires the Schur-cascade integer kernel;
the substrate runs the kernel as the step rule of a single cert-
opcode, with A2 committing the result to the cost ledger. The
mathematical content -- PSD9 of the 9 by 9 NPA matrix at the
witness-derived rationals -- is fully formalised; the bridge from
that PSD condition to physical quantum realizability cites the
external NPA-hierarchy convergence theorem for CHSH (Ishizaka 2025).

What this does not deliver. The result does not formalise the
NPA hierarchy in Coq, does not formalise Q (the physical Tsirelson
set), does not construct a Hilbert space from the moment matrix,
and does not prove Q_{1+AB} = Q. Those are external math facts the
result cites, exactly as every NPA-hierarchy paper cites NPA's own
1+infinity convergence result externally rather than re-formalising
it. The substrate-level content is the cert-opcode discipline plus
the Schur-cascade soundness, which the kernel proves under no
project-local axioms (Print Assumptions reports only
ClassicalDedekindReals.sig_forall_dec and
FunctionalExtensionality.functional_extensionality_dep, the same
two standard-library axioms every other kernel theorem uses).


13. NAME THE STEP YOU REJECT

The construction in Section 5 is the operational test: the buggy
simulator runs on a Turing machine and has no admissible run on a
substrate satisfying A2. The results in Sections 8, 9, 10, 11, and
12 are what the framework lets us state. To dismiss the work, name
which of the following fails.

  1. The five-piece definition of a substrate (S, I, step, c, P).
     Reject by: naming what should be added or removed.

  2. The axiom A2.
     Reject by: explaining why a substrate should permit free
     certification.

  3. The signature claim -- A2 is a property of a step rule only
     when P and c are in the step rule's signature.
     Reject by: making A2 a property of a Turing machine's
     transition function (signature (state, symbol) -> (state,
     symbol, direction)) without extending the signature to include
     P. External annotations do not count -- those make A2 a
     property of (delta, P, c), not of delta. Encoding P and mu
     into the tape alphabet Gamma does not count either -- delta
     still does not consult typed P and c arguments; cost is
     recovered post hoc by reading mu off the tape (see Section 4).
     I claim you cannot.

  4. The subsumption claim -- every classical computation lifts into
     a substrate, and the projection back to classical is
     many-to-one.
     Reject by: producing a classical computation that no substrate
     can host, or a substrate state that projects to no classical
     state.

  5. The structural-undecidability result -- no internally
     representable decider correctly decides AdmitsShortcut on
     arbitrary programs of the substrate.
     Reject by: showing the proof reduces to classical halting under
     inspection, or that the diagonal closes only because of an
     implicit classical-computation assumption.

  6. The cost-ledger observability result -- there exist substrate
     traces with identical classical observable outcomes and
     different cost-ledger outcomes, with the cost gap forced by
     A2 applied to the cert-flipping step.
     Reject by: exhibiting an A2-respecting substrate in which both
     traces have the same cost ledger (which requires either A2 to
     be violated or the construction to be wrong), OR exhibiting a
     function from classical observable to cost ledger that agrees
     with vm_mu on every reachable substrate state.

  7. The quantitative cost lower bound -- for any sound structural
     shortcut narrowing a prior feasible set Omega to a posterior
     Omega' subset of Omega, the substrate trace that realizes the
     shortcut satisfies log_2^up|Omega| - log_2^up|Omega'| <=
     Delta mu. Both sides of the inequality count binary tests /
     cert-setter events on the same trace; mu is lower-bounded by
     the entropy of the narrowing the trace performs.
     Reject by: exhibiting a sound structural shortcut (one
     handing over all the receipts listed in Section 10: strict
     narrowing, distinguishing observation, certified posterior
     predicate, realized-by-the-trace tree, posterior-representative
     reduction) whose trace satisfies Delta mu < log_2^up|Omega| -
     log_2^up|Omega'|. OR showing the realized-by-the-trace
     condition does not pin tree internal nodes to cert-setter
     steps. OR showing A2 does not force cert-setter cost >= 1.
     Note. The bound requires an explicit realized-by-the-trace
     tree as part of the witness; it is not a Shannon bound on
     every deterministic single trace. Pointing out that a single
     trace reaching one leaf pays one cert-event is not a rejection
     -- the file refuses the unconditional single-trace claim at
     monograph/monograph.tex:848.

  8. The polynomial per-step gap between two cost models --
     single-LASSERT certification of a B-bit formula has substrate
     mu-increase at least B + 1, while classical unit-cost RAM time
     is 1 for any single-instruction trace. Between-metric gap:
     factor B + 1.
     Reject by: exhibiting an A2-respecting substrate whose LASSERT
     instruction certifies a B-bit formula at substrate cost less
     than B + 1, OR showing that the substrate's mu-ledger
     conservation law (mu-increase = instruction_cost) is incorrect,
     OR showing that the LASSERT cost rule's bit-scaling is not
     forced by the substrate's design. Pointing out that the gap
     is between two cost models (unit-cost RAM vs substrate mu) is
     not a rejection -- it is the claim (see Section 11). Switching
     classical to bit-cost RAM or Turing-machine time aligns the
     metrics; the substrate then matches classical, which is the
     standard caveat, not a refutation.

  9. The Q_{1+AB} substrate-level certification -- for each of the
     four cert-opcodes in the CHSH_LASSERT_1AB family (gamma = 0,
     gamma_5, gamma_3,4,5, full gamma_1..5), a non-trapping
     execution that does not flip the error latch implies the
     9 by 9 NPA moment matrix is symmetric and PSD at the
     witness-derived rational correlators and bucket-derived
     gamma values.
     Reject by: exhibiting witness counters and gamma buckets for
     which one of the four cert-opcodes does not trap but the
     resulting 9 by 9 NPA moment matrix is NOT PSD, OR showing
     that the Schur-cascade soundness theorem at any slice is
     incorrect (SOS identity wrong, minors witness does not imply
     PSD9, bool decider unsound), OR showing that the soundness
     chain depends on a project-local axiom beyond the two
     standard-library axioms ClassicalDedekindReals.sig_forall_dec
     and FunctionalExtensionality.functional_extensionality_dep.

A wave-off is not a response. Name the step.
